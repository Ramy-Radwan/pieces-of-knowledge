F06 — PWM in 3-phase (the rotating effect)

With one phase, PWM gives you an average voltage. With three phases, the three averages can be arranged so the motor experiences a rotating electrical push.

On this page 1) Three phase voltages 2) Why “rotation” appears 3) Why three-phase PWM becomes tricky 4) Why we need SVM Next

1) Three phase voltages

A 3-phase motor has three windings (A, B, C). The inverter produces three voltages (relative to the DC bus) using ON/OFF switches. Using PWM, each phase can have a controllable average voltage.

Beginner simplification: We “shape” the average A/B/C voltages so the resulting electrical effect behaves like a smooth rotation.

2) Why “rotation” appears

When A/B/C are coordinated properly, the combined magnetic effect in the stator behaves like a single vector that rotates. That rotating effect is what pulls the rotor around.

You do not need to memorize waveforms now. Soon we will replace three signals (A, B, C) with one picture (the αβ plane).

3) Why three-phase PWM becomes tricky

In one phase, duty cycle directly sets an average. In three phases, you must choose switching patterns that:

  • produce the desired 3-phase averages
  • stay within what the inverter can physically do
  • avoid ugly ripple and wasted voltage capability

This is where “smart modulation” comes in.

4) Why we need SVM

Space Vector Modulation (SVM) is the clean geometric method to do PWM in a 3-phase inverter.

SVM lets us talk about “a desired voltage vector” instead of three separate waveforms, and it uses a smart switching strategy to produce that vector efficiently.
