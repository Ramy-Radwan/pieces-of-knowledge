F03 — Voltage’s job: how current changes

If torque is mostly about current, then the next question is: how do we influence current? The practical answer is: we apply voltage.

On this page 1) Voltage “pushes” current 2) Three reasons you need voltage 3) Why higher speed needs more voltage Next

1) Voltage “pushes” current

A motor phase winding is not just a wire. Electrically, it behaves like:

  • Resistance (R): causes a voltage drop when current flows.
  • Inductance (L): resists fast changes of current.
  • Back-EMF: a “generated voltage” that increases with speed and fights your applied voltage.
Beginner takeaway:
Voltage is what gives you the ability to change current (and keep it at the level you want).

2) Three reasons you need voltage

When you apply voltage, it is “spent” in three places:

  • R drop: part of voltage is used just to overcome resistance (heating loss).
  • L drop: part of voltage is needed to change current quickly (dynamic behavior).
  • Back-EMF: at speed, the motor generates a voltage that opposes you.

We will later show the classic equation style (V = R·I + L·dI/dt + back-EMF), but you do not need it yet. The idea is enough.

3) Why higher speed needs more voltage

At low speed, the motor generates only a small back-EMF. So it is relatively easy to push current.

At high speed, back-EMF becomes large. It is like the motor is “pushing back” electrically. To maintain the same current (and torque), you often need more applied voltage.

This is one big reason why the voltage magnitude in SVM/FOC cannot always be “maximum” or “constant”. It must adapt to speed and load.
