F07 — The αβ plane (one rotating vector)

Three-phase systems are often described with three signals (A, B, C). That is correct, but hard to “see”. The αβ plane gives us a simple picture.

On this page 1) One perfect sentence 2) Why we use αβ 3) How αβ relates to SVM and FOC Next

1) One perfect sentence

The αβ plane is a 2D coordinate system that represents three-phase electrical quantities as a single vector, giving a clean geometric view that is easier to visualize and reason about.

You do not need to derive anything yet. We use αβ as a picture first, and the math later (only if needed).

2) Why we use αβ

If you stare at three waveforms (A, B, C), it is hard to “see” the overall direction and strength. In αβ, you can often describe the same situation as:

  • Angle = where the vector points (electrical direction).
  • Magnitude = how strong the vector is (how strong the electrical push is).

This is exactly why SVM diagrams are drawn in αβ: it makes the switching limits and the desired vector visible.

3) How αβ relates to SVM and FOC

αβ is not “owned” by SVM or FOC. It is a shared picture used across motor control:

  • SVM uses αβ to show what voltages the inverter can synthesize (the hexagon) and how to approximate a desired vector.
  • FOC uses αβ (and dq) to control torque-producing currents in a clean rotating reference frame.
If you are going into SVM next: αβ helps you see the hexagon and corner vectors.
If you are going into FOC next: αβ helps you describe rotating quantities before moving into dq.
