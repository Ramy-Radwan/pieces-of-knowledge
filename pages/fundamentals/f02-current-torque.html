F02 — Why current creates torque (push strength)

Torque in electric motors comes from magnetic “push”. Current is what creates and strengthens that push.

On this page 1) The core idea (no math) 2) Why controlling current matters 3) What voltage has to do with it Next

1) The core idea (no math)

Most motors have:

  • A magnetic field on the rotor (permanent magnets, or an energized rotor winding).
  • A magnetic field created by currents in the stator windings.

When these magnetic fields interact, the rotor experiences a turning force: torque.

Beginner rule of thumb:
More current → stronger magnetic effect → more torque (up to limits).

So when you ask the motor for “more push”, what you are really asking for is: more current in the right direction.

2) Why controlling current matters

Current is powerful — and dangerous if uncontrolled:

  • Too much current → heating → damage.
  • Current spikes → noise and vibration.
  • Unstable current → unstable torque → ugly speed behavior.

This is why advanced control methods (especially FOC) often focus on controlling current tightly. But even simple BLDC control still uses PWM duty to influence current.

3) What voltage has to do with it

Here is the next key:

We do not “set current directly” like a knob.
We usually control voltage, and the motor’s electrical physics decides how current changes.
