F01 — Speed vs Torque (the real goal)

Before SVM and FOC, we must be clear about the goal. Motor control is mainly about controlling torque and speed.

On this page 1) Two knobs: torque and speed 2) Real-life examples 3) How this connects to SVM/FOC Next

1) Two knobs: torque and speed

Think of a motor like a person pushing a door:

  • Torque = how hard you push (push strength).
  • Speed = how fast it rotates (how quickly it turns).

Most of the time, we do not want “maximum everything”. We want the motor to do a specific job: accelerate, maintain speed, hold position, move smoothly, not overheat, not exceed current limits, etc.

Simple mental model:
– Torque makes the motor accelerate (or resist a load).
– Speed is the result after acceleration continues for some time.
– If you keep pushing hard (high torque), speed keeps rising until something limits it.

2) Real-life examples

  • Starting from rest: you need torque to overcome inertia and start moving.
  • Going uphill / heavy load: you need more torque to keep the same speed.
  • Holding constant speed: you adjust torque just enough to match the load.
  • Braking: you apply negative torque to reduce speed.

In electric vehicles, drones, robots, and industrial drives, “good control” means the motor produces exactly the torque needed — not random maximum power.

3) How this connects to SVM/FOC

To control torque and speed, we need to control the motor’s electrical behavior. The key chain is:

Torque comes from current → current is influenced by voltage → voltage is created by inverter switching + PWM.
