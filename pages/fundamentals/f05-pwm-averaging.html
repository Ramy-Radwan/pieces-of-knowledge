F05 — PWM as averaging (1 phase)

The inverter cannot “dial” a voltage smoothly. It can only switch ON/OFF. PWM is the trick that turns ON/OFF into a controllable average voltage.

On this page 1) The problem: only two levels 2) The idea: fast switching + averaging 3) Duty cycle (the simple knob) 4) Why the motor “feels” the average Next

1) The problem: only two levels

Imagine you have only two possible voltages you can apply:

OFF → 0 V
ON → Vdc (the DC bus voltage)

But you might want something in the middle, like “half strength”. The inverter cannot output half instantly. It can only output ON or OFF.

2) The idea: fast switching + averaging

PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) does this:

  • Switch ON for a short time
  • Switch OFF for a short time
  • Repeat very fast

If ON happens 50% of the time, the average behaves like “half voltage”. If ON happens 20% of the time, the average behaves like “small voltage”.

3) Duty cycle (the simple knob)

The “percentage of ON time” in each PWM cycle is called duty cycle.

Duty = 0% → average voltage ~ 0
Duty = 50% → average voltage ~ 0.5 × Vdc
Duty = 100% → average voltage ~ Vdc

This is exactly why your SVM demo has a magnitude slider: it represents “how much voltage” we want on average.

4) Why the motor “feels” the average

Two reasons:

  • Inductance in the motor windings smooths current changes (current cannot jump instantly).
  • Mechanical inertia smooths torque and speed (the rotor cannot jump instantly either).

So even though the inverter output is “jumpy” instant-by-instant, the motor responds mostly to the short-time average.
