SVM 03 — Sector detection + mapping to switching states

In SVM 02 we saw the “magic picture”: two neighbor corner vectors + a zero vector, timed by T1/T2/T0. Now we answer the very practical question: how do we know which two corners to use, and what do those corners mean inside the inverter switches?

On this page 1) Sector detection (which wedge am I in?) 2) The two neighbors (Vx and Vy) 3) Switch states (100, 110, …) explained simply 4) Why two zero vectors (V0 and V7) Next

1) Sector detection (which wedge am I in?)

The SVM hexagon is split into 6 equal wedges (sectors), each 60° wide. Your desired vector angle determines which sector you are currently in.

Sector rule:
Sector 1: 0° .. 60°
Sector 2: 60° .. 120°
...
Sector 6: 300° .. 360°

So “sector detection” is basically: which 60° slice contains my desired vector angle?

If angle feels abstract, revisit: F07 — αβ plane.

2) The two neighbors (Vx and Vy)

Once you know the sector, the two corner vectors are fixed: they are simply the two corners that form that sector.

Example: if you are in Sector 1 (0°..60°), the two corners are V1 and V2.
If you are in Sector 2 (60°..120°), the two corners are V2 and V3.
And so on.

Then SVM chooses times T1 and T2 to “mix” these two corners so their average points to your desired vector. (That was the SVM 02 diagram.)

If T1/T2/T0 is still fuzzy, open: SVM 02.

3) Switch states (100, 110, …) explained simply

The inverter has three phase outputs (A, B, C). At any instant, each phase is connected either to +DC or to −DC (by the top/bottom switch).

A common way to describe this is with three bits:

Switch state “abc”
a = 1 means phase A is connected to +DC (top switch ON)
a = 0 means phase A is connected to −DC (bottom switch ON)
Same for b (phase B) and c (phase C).

So:

  • 100 means A is high (+DC), B low (−DC), C low (−DC).
  • 110 means A high, B high, C low.
  • 010 means A low, B high, C low.
  • … etc.

These are not “random”. They are the only instant voltage possibilities the inverter can create because it is built from ON/OFF switches. (If this still feels new, read: F04 — inverter basics.)

Each non-zero switch state corresponds to a corner vector in the hexagon. Example mapping (common convention):

V1 ↔ 100
V2 ↔ 110
V3 ↔ 010
V4 ↔ 011
V5 ↔ 001
V6 ↔ 101

Don’t worry if you don’t memorize this now. The important point is: each corner is a real, physical switch pattern.

4) Why two zero vectors (V0 and V7)

There are two special switch states:

  • 000 (all phases low)
  • 111 (all phases high)

In both cases, the motor sees “no line-to-line voltage” from the inverter, so these are called zero vectors. In SVM, we use a zero vector for time T0 to complete the PWM cycle.

This is why many SVM sequences are written like: V0 → Vx → Vy → V7 → Vy → Vx → V0
